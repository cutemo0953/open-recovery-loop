# Red Flags by Phase — TKA Standard Protocol

> DISCLAIMER: This content is provided as a reference framework for clinical education and software development. It does NOT constitute medical advice. Actual clinical decisions must be made by qualified healthcare professionals based on individual patient assessment.

---

## Overview

Red flags are clinical findings that warrant immediate attention, reassessment, or escalation to the surgeon. They are organized by rehabilitation phase because the expected clinical picture changes over time — what is normal at Week 1 may be abnormal at Week 8.

---

## Phase 0 — Prehab (Week -8 to 0)

| Red Flag | Action |
|----------|--------|
| Significant ROM loss compared to baseline | Reassess — possible new pathology |
| Acute pain increase without injury | Medical evaluation before surgery |
| Signs of infection in the operative knee | Delay surgery, refer to surgeon |
| Uncontrolled medical comorbidities | Medical clearance reassessment |

---

## Phase 1 — Acute (Week 0 to 2)

| Red Flag | Action |
|----------|--------|
| VAS pain > 8/10 at rest despite medication | Evaluate for compartment syndrome, wound complication, or DVT |
| Excessive wound drainage or dehiscence | Immediate surgeon notification |
| Calf pain, swelling, positive Homan's sign | Rule out DVT — urgent vascular assessment |
| Fever > 38.5 C | Evaluate for surgical site infection or systemic infection |
| Inability to achieve any active quad contraction | Evaluate for nerve injury or patellar tendon disruption |
| Knee locked in flexion, unable to extend | Evaluate for mechanical block (loose body, hematoma) |
| Rapid, tense effusion (tense hemarthrosis) | Aspirate, evaluate for vascular injury or coagulopathy |

---

## Phase 2 — Subacute (Week 2 to 6)

| Red Flag | Action |
|----------|--------|
| ROM flexion < 75 degrees at Week 4 | Risk of arthrofibrosis — intensify ROM program, consider MUA |
| Progressive loss of ROM (previously gained ROM decreasing) | Urgent reassessment — possible arthrofibrosis or complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS) |
| Persistent wound drainage beyond 3 weeks | Rule out infection — wound culture, CRP/ESR |
| New onset of instability (giving way) | Evaluate collateral ligaments, component position |
| CRPS signs: disproportionate pain, color changes, allodynia, edema | Early referral to pain management |
| Persistent moderate-to-large effusion not improving | Joint aspiration, infection workup |

---

## Phase 3 — Strengthening (Week 6 to 12)

| Red Flag | Action |
|----------|--------|
| ROM flexion plateau < 100 degrees | Risk of permanent stiffness — surgeon consultation for possible MUA or lysis of adhesions |
| New onset pain after period of improvement | Evaluate for stress fracture, component loosening, or new injury |
| Persistent limp despite adequate ROM | Evaluate hip abductor strength, leg length discrepancy, pain avoidance pattern |
| Clicking, catching, or locking | Evaluate for component issue, loose body, or patellar maltracking |
| Extension deficit > 5 degrees not improving | Aggressive extension protocol, consider posterior capsule stretching |

---

## Phase 4 — Return to Function (Week 12 to 26)

| Red Flag | Action |
|----------|--------|
| New pain onset at rest | Evaluate for late infection, component loosening, or periprosthetic fracture |
| Progressive ROM loss | X-ray to rule out component migration or heterotopic ossification |
| Persistent anterior knee pain | Evaluate patellar tracking, component rotation, and quadriceps loading |
| Giving way or instability with activity | Evaluate ligament balance and component alignment |
| Sudden sharp pain with audible pop | Evaluate for periprosthetic fracture or extensor mechanism rupture |

---

## Universal Red Flags (Any Phase)

These warrant immediate escalation regardless of the rehabilitation phase:

| Red Flag | Action |
|----------|--------|
| Signs of DVT/PE: calf pain, sudden dyspnea, chest pain | Emergency evaluation |
| Surgical site infection: erythema, warmth, purulent drainage, fever | Urgent surgeon referral |
| Periprosthetic fracture: sudden severe pain after fall or trauma | Emergency X-ray and surgeon consultation |
| Neurovascular compromise: numbness, foot drop, absent pulses | Urgent evaluation |
| Rapid large effusion with systemic symptoms | Joint aspiration, infection workup |
